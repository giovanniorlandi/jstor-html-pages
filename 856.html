Warfare and torture occur on a large scale\nin many countries resulting in widespread death, disability,\nand trauma [1]. In 1997, Amnesty International reported\nthat human rights violations had been recorded in over\n150 countries worldwide [2]. Thus, the number of civilians\naffected by war, in comparison to military personnel, is rising\nbecause of the amount of cases of interpersonal violence\n[3]. According to the latest statistics of the United Nations\nHigh Commissioner for Refugees, there are approximately\n42 million “forcibly displaced persons” worldwide of whom\n15.2 million were refugees [4]. Those living in a war-affected\narea experience a range of severely traumatic experiences\n[5, 6]. For example, in the Democratic Republic of Congo,\nwhere a second civil war has been existent since 1998, it is\ncommon for civilians to be killed, kidnapped, sexually\nabused, enslaved, or tortured at gunpoint [7, 8]. Feeling\nthat one’s life is in danger, witnessing extreme violence or\nan individual’s death, separation from ones family, or being\ndetained against one’s will, are diverse factors which can contribute\nto the development of various psychiatric diseases.\nThis systematic review will focus on Posttraumatic Stress\nDisorder (PTSD), the most commonly studied consequence\nin relation to the atrocities previously cited.\nPTSD was first recognized following the devastating war\nexperiences of soldiers serving in Vietnam. Since then, the\nconcept has been adequately applied in the assessment of\nvarious types of traumatic experience [9]. Most studies\nreported high prevalence rates of PTSD among tortured\nrefugees (31% to 92%) and refugees affected by war trauma\n(23.5 to 77%) [9]. Unfortunately, there is limited\nand disparate information on what intervention is the most\nappropriate and effective for this category of victims. The\nprimary objective of this systematic review is to assess\nwhether the different forms of CBT can successfully help\nadult civilians (specifically women) who experienced waror-conflict-related\ntrauma (imprisonment, torture, sexual\nabuse, rape, kidnapping, or detainment against will) cope\nwith the symptoms of PTSD and depression. Secondary, we\nidentify which form of CBT is more efficient in reducing the\nseverity of the previously cited outcomes.\n1.2. PTSD and Different Forms of CBT. Given the clinical\ncomplexity of PTSD, it is not surprising that the development\nof treatments is quite challenging. Cognitive-behavioral\ntherapy (CBT) includes a number of diverse but related techniques\nsuch as exposure therapy, stress inoculation training,\ncognitive processing therapy, cognitive therapy, relaxation\ntraining, dialectical behavior therapy and acceptance, and\ncommitment therapy [13].\nExposure therapy (ET) refers to a series of procedures\ndesigned to help individuals confront thoughts, and safe or\nlow-risk stimuli, that are feared or avoided [13]. Applied\nto the treatment of PTSD, most exposure therapy programs\ninclude imaginal exposure to the trauma memory and in\nvivo exposure to reminders of the trauma or triggers for\ntrauma-related fear and avoidance [13]. In 2002, Neuner’s\nresearch team developed a new form of ET called Narrative\nExposure Therapy (NET). NET is a standardized short-term\napproach in which the classical form of ET is adapted to\nmeet the needs of traumatized survivors of war and torture\n[14]. As most of the victims of organized violence have\nexperienced many traumatic events, it is often impossible\nfor them to identify the worst event before treatment. To\novercome this difficulty, the team combined with ET, the\ntestimony therapy’s approach of Lira and Weinstein designed\nto treat traumatized survivors of the Pinochet regime in Chile\n[15]. Instead of defining a single event as a target in therapy,\nthe patient constructs a narration of his whole life from birth\nup to the present situation while focusing on the detailed\nreport of the traumatic experiences [14]. This treatment\nhas shown low dropout rates in different studies. Many\nthink that the main motivator of NET is the anticipation\nof receiving a written biography upon completion, that can\nhelp participants pass on their story to their children, while\nsimultaneously educating them [16].\nStress Inoculation Training (SIT) is a multicomponent\nanxiety management treatment program that includes education,\nmuscle relaxation training, breathing retraining, role\nplaying, covert modeling, guided self-dialogue, and thought\nstopping [13]. Cognitive therapy (CT) predicated the idea\nthat it is one’s interpretation of an event rather than the\nevent itself that determines emotional reactions. It involves\nidentifying erroneous or unhelpful cognitions, evaluating the\nevidence for and against these cognitions, and considering\nwhether the cognitions are the result of cognitive biases\nor errors, in the service of developing more realistic or\nuseful cognitions [13]. Cognitive processing therapy (CPT)\nimplements exposure to the trauma memory via writing a\ntrauma narrative and repeatedly reading it, and is combined\nwith CT focused on themes of safety, trust, power/control,\nesteem, and intimacy [13]. A culturallyadapted form of\nCPT was developed in 2004 by a team of researchers from\nMassachusetts to fit the needs of traumatised Cambodian\nrefugees with pharmacotherapy-resistant PTSD [17]. Dialectical\nbehavior therapy (DBT) is a comprehensive treatment\ndeveloped for the treatment of individuals with borderline\npersonality disorder. An important aspect of DBT is skills\ntraining in affect regulation and interpersonal regulation.\nSome trauma survivors may have deficits in these skill areas\nthat render it difficult for them to tolerate or benefit from\ntrauma-focused interventions such as ET [13].\nCBT is the most studied treatments in the general\npopulation, and current guidelines recommend it as a firstline\ntreatment for patients [13]. The different forms of CBT\nhave been studied as treatment for PTSD since the early\n1960s [18]. However, the amount and quality of evidence\nvaries substantially from a program to another. CBT’s\ndifferent forms have been recognised as effective in helping\nsurvivors of trauma resulting from accidents, rape and crime\nwitnessing; however, their effectiveness in helping war-orconflict-related\ntraumatised civilians is yet to be proven.\n2. Methods\n2.1. Search Methods for Identification of Studies. The recommendations\nof the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic\nReviews of Interventions were followed in the design of the\nsearch strategy. The first author worked in collaboration\nwith professionals at the Paramedical library of University\nof Montreal. Their role was to evaluate the research strategy\nand advise on the possible missed channels of search. The\nCochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL),\n', 'The Scientific World Journal 3\nCINAHL, EMBASE, Entrez-Pubmed; PsycInfo, Web of Science,\nMEDLINE (OvidSP), and USA clinical trials databases were\nsearched. In addition, the dissertation and thesis database\nwas also reviewed for relevant publications. The keywords\nrelated to trauma (PTSD, posttraumatic stress disorders,\nstress disorders) were first used to extract publications on\nthe subject. In order to refine the results to the population\nof interest (civilians, adults), studies conducted with the\ncombatants (with keywords veterans and military personnel)\nand the young traumatised civilians (with keywords\nadolescent∗, youth∗, child∗) were excluded. Since a lot\nof the interventions in countries affected by war/conflict\nare conducted by diverse humanitarian organisations (i.e.,\nWHO, IANSA, UNHCR), these organisation websites were\nscanned for useful publications on the subject. A manual\nsearch was also performed using the reference list of reviews\nand books published on PTSD treatment in the population\nof interest [13, 19, 20]. This step was completed by searching\npotential publications with the keywords related to each\nform of CBT (exposure therapy, stress inoculation training,\ncognitive processing therapy, etc.).\n2.2. Criteria for Considering Studies for This Paper. The inclusion\ncriteria consisted of studies describing a randomised\ncontrolled trial in which the intervention compared any\nform of CBT to a control (no treatment, delayed treatment,\ntreatment as usual, or non-CBT psychotherapy). The study\nneeded to be designed to reduce PTSD symptoms among\ncivilians who experienced one or multiple trauma during\nwar-or-conflict. Study participants were required to be adults\nwith the status of refugees, asylum seekers, or internally\ndisplaced. Trials conducted with participants traumatised\nby war/conflict related violence who were still living in\ntheir country of origin were also included. Studies with\nparticipants with comorbidities were accepted provided the\nprimary objective of the trial was the reduction of PTSD\nsymptoms. The main comorbid conditions found in the\nincluded studies were depression, anxiety, suicidal ideations,\nneck-focused and orthostasis-triggered panic attacks, with\nflashbacks during attacks and somatoform disorders. Most\nof the studies excluded participants with organic mental\ndisorder, bipolar disorder, mental retardation, schizophrenia,\npsychosis, and drug abuse. The instruments used for the\nassessment of PTSD symptoms and severity had to be based\non DSM’s or ICD’s criteria for a study to be eligible.\n2.3. Data Collection and Analysis. The Cochrane Handbook\nfor Systematic Reviews of interventions [21] was used to\ndevelop the methods of this paper. All studies resulting from\nsearch strategy were assessed for potential inclusion. They\nwere excluded if the randomization process was incomplete,\nthere was no control group or no data was reported. The\nstudies in which civilians and army/police officers were\ncombined were also excluded because of the different nature\nin which they are exposed to trauma. A data extraction form\nwas designed. The first author performed the initial data\nextraction, and the Review Manager software (RevMan 5)\nwas used to enter the data a second time. The articles’ authors\nwere contacted for further details on their studies if the trial\nconditions or data reported were unclear.\n2.4. Assessment of Risk of Bias in Included Studies. The first\nauthor assessed the risk of bias in included studies considering\ncriteria outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for\nSystematic Reviews of Interventions [22] and the second\nauthor reviewed the assessment for accuracy. Methods used\nfor generation of the randomization sequence were described\nfor each trial if reported. To assess the bias introduced\ninto each study, five key domains were considered: sequence\ngeneration, allocation concealment, blinding of participants,\nblinding of assessors, and completeness of outcome data.\n2.5. Missing Data. Data was analysed with the available information\nfor each group participants according to their\nallocation.\n2.6. Data Synthesis. A meta-analysis method with a randomeffect\nwas chosen since different instruments were used to\nassess PTSD or depression severity in the included trials. In\nfact, the use of a random-effects meta-analysis allows the\nincorporation of the heterogeneity existing among studies.\nTwo subgroup analyses were conducted with the Review\nManager software (RevMan 5): the first assessed the effects\nof the treatments on PTSD score and the second assessed the\neffect of the treatments on depression score. The subgroup\nanalyses also compared one form of CBT to the other. Each\nanalysis investigates potential sources of heterogeneity, as\ndifferences in the type of intervention, participants’ profile,\nor intervention setting (e.g., length and number of sessions)\nmay affect the treatment effects. Potential Biases in the Review Process\nFirst, the selection of a specific category of war-traumatised\npatients (adults) might seem arbitrary. We thought that it is\nimportant to separate adults from young ones because the\ntype of CBT and NET treatment used in those categories\nare not the same. We also separated those categories of age\nbecause we felt that the process of healing might be different\nbetween an adult who is usually the victim of violence and\na child/adolescent who is usually a witness and/or victim\nof that violence. Secondly, although an extensive list of\ndatabases was used for study selection, we cannot rule out the\npossibility that some articles may have been missed with our\nresearch strategy. In fact, since five out of ten of the studies\nwere conducted by German researchers we cannot exclude\nthe fact that some of their studies were not found in the\nEnglish databases used.\n6. Main Conclusions of the Study and\nImplications for Practice\nThis systematic review demonstrates that culturallyadapted\nCPT, CPT, and NET can successfully help war-or-conflicttraumatised\ncivilians in reducing their PTSD symptoms.\nHowever, only the culturallyadapted CPT seems effective in\nreducing the depression score of civilians who experienced\nwar-or-conflict related violence. Even if the subgroup analysis\nclearly demonstrates that CPT and its culturallyadapted\nform are more effective than NET, more evidence is needed\nin order to specifically recommend one of the forms of CBT\nover the other for our population of interest.\n7. Implications for Research\n7.1. Recommendations for Study Designs. More research\nneeds to be carried out on CBT (specifically its culturallyadapted\nform) and NET treatment effectiveness in PTSD\nseverity reduction within our population of interest. In fact,\nthese people need to cope with the symptoms, in order to\nbe able to contribute to the prosperity and well-being of\ntheir societies. It is also important that researchers agree\non common characteristics which will allow conduct of a\nbetter comparison between studies in the future, in order to\nrecommend a specific treatment. They should agree on the\ninstruments, the number of sessions given, and their length.\nFor example, in the CBT trials the number of sessions was\ndifferent from one study to another. It is also important\nthat the same type of control group be used for each trial\n(delayed-treatment or treatment as usual). In fact, there\nwas a difference in the type of control group between the\ntrials that used NET as an intervention. Trials comparing\nthese two methods to a standard treatment might also be\na good idea and collaboration between the varying teams\n(Hinton and Neuner teams) would certainly help this. To\nensure the quality of the trials, researchers must also be\nsure to conceal allocation (none of the reviewed studies\ndid so) and blind the assessors (followed by a test of\nthe blindness integrity). Moreover, researchers must make\ncertain that enough participants in the intervention and\ncontrol group are included in the trial. In fact, a key\nlimitation in most studies is the sample size. Only one study\nincluded an adequate number of participants (n = 111) in\nthe intervention group, but also reported a high number of\nlosses to followup, which made the results partly subject to\nthe risk of bias such as incomplete outcome data.\n7.2. Integrating Cultural Validity. There is not only a question\nof cultural validity regarding the PTSD itself (and its associated\ncomorbidities), but also the instruments and specialists\nused to assess it remain important. In fact, some argue\nthat PTSD symptoms may have various values or meanings\ndepending on the different cultures, and some symptoms\nmay not be perceived as distressing among certain groups\n[37]. The validity of applying a western-based trauma model,\nand the label PTSD to people from nonwestern countries\nhas also been questioned [38]. Summerfield has even raised\n', 'The Scientific World Journal 17\nsome fierce criticisms on the programs that do not integrate\nthe question of validity when working with nonwestern populations.\nSurprisingly, none of the NET trials included lead\nresearchers originating from the refugee population, a situation\nthat most likely reduces the possibility of including the\naspect of cultural validity in the intervention.\n7.3. Research on Other Promising Treatments. Aside from\nNET and CPT, different therapies have been studied in our\npopulation of interest. One of them, testimonial psychotherapy,\nwas used as treatment for the intervention group in\nan RCT [39]. This intervention was conducted with nondisplaced\nMozambican civilians who had experienced trauma\nduring a civil war in Mozambique. Several writers have\nnoted that testimony psychotherapy offers survivors some\ntherapeutic benefits in their trauma recovery [40]. As NET, it\nallows participants to have a written testimony at the end of\nthe treatment with a benefit of a high completion rate. Family\ngroup therapy has also been described as potentially efficient\nin the population of interest. This treatment might be even\nmore effective when several patients or traumatized persons\nbelong to the same family, a situation that is fairly common\n[41–43]. This therapy gives the patients the advantage to\nreceive social support from their family which facilitates their\naccess and use of mental health services.
