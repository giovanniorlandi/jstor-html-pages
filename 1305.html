One reason for the variation can \nbe traced to the level of economic development for a particular economy in a particular time \nperiod. For example, developed economies rely more on high value-added industrial and services \nsectors to generate employment and income, whereas developing economies rely more on \nagriculture and natural resource extraction to generate employment and income. Residents in \ndeveloped economies generally enjoy a higher standard of living than residents in developing \neconomies. And because there is a lot of catching up to do (e.g., many “need” gaps in developing \neconomies that require closing—infrastructure, critical services, and availability of consumer \ndurables), developing economies, when they get their acts together, tend to experience faster \neconomic growth than developed economies. As an economy matures and its residents reach a \nhigh standard of living, its growth rate tends to fall to the level experienced in other developed \neconomies.  \n \nAnother reason for some of the variations in economic performance between countries \nand over time is the result of economic policy. Some countries, as a matter of policy, are more \nopen to international trade and international investment, whereas others put up more barriers to \nlimit international trade (e.g., import duties) and international investment (e.g., limits on foreign \ninvestments). For countries that are more open, the growth in international trade and foreign \ninvestments can contribute significantly to economic growth.  \n \nA third reason for some of the variation in economic performance between countries and \nover time is the intensity of investment. Some economies, often rapidly growing developing \neconomies, devote a large proportion of their GDP to investment—to build plants, purchase \nequipment, and construct bridges and highways—to increase the stock of capital available for the \nproduction of goods and services.  \n \nOf course, there are many other reasons for observed variations in reported economic \nperformance. Political stability is important for economic development. Investors would not \nwant to undertake long-term projects in an economy if they expected a civil war to break out in \nthe near future. The efficiency of economic institutions is also important.
