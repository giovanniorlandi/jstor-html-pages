The translation \ntechniques applied are transcoding; borrowing from the English or other \nEuropean languages; descriptive translation (explicitation); literal \ntranslation; neutralization; approximate translation; the use of translational \ndoublets; contextual translation; omission. H. Savory points out an important demand put to the \ntranslation of the fiction that describes the past epochs specifying that the target text should \nread as a contemporary of the original and a contemporary of the translator, i.e. the target \nreader at the same time [5: 49]. On the other hand, in a historical novel lexical, \nmorphological and syntactical archaic forms aim at creating a special stylistic effect of a \ndefinite epoch [6: 142]. Hence, the problem faced by the translators is whether to bring the \ntranslated text closer to their own time and target cultural community ignoring historical \nand archaic words or use corresponding words of the target language in order to preserve \nthe atmosphere of the epoch reflected in the original text. After all, there is another option \nwhen both approaches are combined and balanced. This is especially important, as the shift \nto the excessive use of archaisms and historicisms in the translation can make the text \nabsolutely obscured, whereas omitting the lexical units mentioned above will deprive the \ntext of its historical and cultural flavour and colouring. \nThere exists a well-established tradition connected with the research of culture-specific \nitems in fiction in Ukrainian [7, 8] and foreign translation theory [9], but the problem is far \nfrom being solved as there is no unanimity even as to the term itself. Bulgarian and PostSoviet\ntheories of translation studies are based on the notion of realia, Ukrainian реалія, \nRussian реалия [10], whereas American, Asian and Western European traditions offer a \nnumber of terms, such as cultural words [11], culture-bound problems [12], culture-bound \nconcepts [13], culture-specific terms [14], culture-specific items [15]. What unites different \napproaches is the thought that cultural information is one of the sources of untranslatability \n[16] and is a challenge for translators.  \nWe argue that historical culture-specific items employed by the writers in fiction \ncomprise the cultural code, which is defined as a way of structuring cultural knowledge [17: \n20], a set of images and ideas that are ethnically and culturally determined and are \nverbalized by means of a language in the literary work. The translator’s task is to render the \ncultural code without distorting it. The latter is problematic, as the receiver can impose his \nor her own artistic language on the source text and re-code it [18: 25] in the process of \ntranslation. \nWhen it comes to culture-specific items representation in translation, notwithstanding \ntheir low translatability [19: 223], there exists a definite tradition to render them in \ntranslation with the help of transcoding or borrowing, calque, description, approximation, \ntransformation [20: 7], renomination [7], adaptation [21] to compensate for the lack of \nequivalence [16: 73].  \nMost culture-specific items in a historical novel are represented by archaisms and \nhistoricisms that reflect the atmosphere of the epoch where the scene is laid. Such lexical \nunits can be used in order to render the corresponding items of the source text or they can \nbe applied in the contexts when there are no archaisms and historicisms in the source text.  \nResearch methods of the paper comprise textual analysis aiming at specifying the \nstructure of the source and target texts; contextual analysis helping to define the historical \nand cultural atmosphere of the source text; translational analysis allowing to single out \ntechniques used by the translators while rendering culture-specific items in Ukrianian and \nRussian; the analysis of dictionary definitions making it possible to identify the meaning of \nthe culture-specific items in the source text and the lexemes used to render them in the \ntarget texts; contrastive analysis enabling us to state the differences in the process of \npresentation of culture-specific items in English, Ukrainian and Russian. \n3 Results and Discussion \nThe culture-specific items denoting status of a person in the novel Gone with the Wind \nverbalise the cultural code of the text. In this study we differentiate the lexical units naming \npeople, mainly the inhabitants of the USA, according to their background, political and \nreligious beliefs, social layer, occupation, ethnic and Afro-American identity.  \nAs the novel describes the struggle of the North and the South from the point of view of \nthe Southerners, the author uses nominations of the inhabitants of these regions. The Russian language borrowed the \nlexeme янки in the meaning of уроженец или житель Новой Англии (северовосточных \nштатов США) [24: 945]. As it is seen from the dictionary definitions the Ukrainian \nborrowed lexeme янкi has the semes ‘place of living’ and ‘time’, whereas the Russian \nequivalent demonstrates the presence of the semes ‘place of living’, ‘place of birth’ being \nnearer in meaning to the English lexeme. The technique of transcoding is found in the \nRussian target text as well: Да и вообще, янки слишком боятся нас, чтобы решиться с \nнами воевать [3: 19]. Mitchell and their rendering in the target texts in \nUkranian and Russian makes it clear that such items play a significant role in creating a \nhistorical atmosphere in the source and target texts. The research proves that culturespecific\nitems denoting status are divided into those naming the inhabitants of the USA \naccording to their background, political and religious views, social stratification, \noccupation, ethnic or Afro-American identity. The above mentioned lexemes verbalise the \ncultural code of the novel. The problems with their representation in the translations are \nconnected with the fact that they do not always have direct correspondences in the \nlanguages of the target texts. As a result, the translator resorts to a number of translation \ntechniques, such as transcoding and borrowing from the English or other European \nlanguages alone or combined with descriptive translation (elicitation); literal translation \nwith the help of a native lexeme of the target language; descriptive translation \n(explicitation); the loss of stylistic colouring, neutralization; approximate translation with \nthe help of a partial equivalent; the use of the translational doublet; contextual translation, \nand omission. The research proves that in the majority of cases the Ukrainian and Russian \ntranslations demonstrate the choice of the similar techniques, the rare differences lie in the \nfact that the Ukrainian variant strives for the description combined with transcoding in \norder to make the meaning clearer to the reader, whereas the Russian variant is limited by \ntranscoding alone. Besides, there are cases when one of the translations shows \nneutralisation and the loss of the stylistic effect, whereas the other manages to preserve it. \nThe further perspectives of the study are connected with defining the specifics of the \ntranslation techniques used in rendering historical culture-specific items from Ukrainian \ninto English. \nReferences \n1.
