['AGRONOMIC AFFAIRS I 7 5  \nIn the Tennessee Valley there are thousands of farms where the operating \nprogram for several years past is a matter of record. Here the soils and their \ncharacteristics have been mapped and, to a large degree, their adaptability deter- \nmined. If these farms are representative of those in the rest of the United States, \nsuitable nitrogen fertilizers (properly supplemented with other elements) can be \nused in very large quantities-perhaps as much as may be available after the \nwar. Heretofore, when nitrogen was scarce, it was important to get the maximum \ncrop production per unit of nitrogen; when nitrogen gets plentiful, it becomes \nimportant to get the maximum production per acre of land suitable for intensive \nproduction.
